The Bots

Each bot tags every order with its own client_order_id and only ever sells what it bought itself — three books, politely sharing accounts without stepping on each other.

Stock account · hourly bars

RSI Bot rsibot-*

The original. Watches TSLA, NVDA, PLTR on 1-hour bars and buys fear: when RSI(14) drops below 25 (deeply oversold) it market-buys $5,000, then exits when RSI recovers above 50 — or bails at an 8% stop-loss. The config won a tournament backtest against 10 rival strategies. A challenger (RSI 30/55, no stop) beat it in-sample and out-of-sample once — one more monthly win and it takes the crown under the promotion rule.

RSI(14) <25 buy>50 sell stop −8%$5k/entry 1h bars

Stock account · daily bars

Bot v2.1 botv2-*

The portfolio manager. Scans a ~150-name universe (mega-cap tech core, an AI/semis watchlist, and broad US large-caps) for statistical dips — a close 2σ below its 20-day mean — but only buys names above their 200-day average, and only while SPY itself is in an uptrend. Entries are volatility-sized ($1–3k), take profit back at the mean, stop at −10%, or time-stop after 21 days. It also keeps a 16-name core equal-weighted and benchmarks itself against a "frozen twin" of its own day-one holdings.

dip z < −2trend 200d MA vol-sized $1–3kTP 20d mean stop −10%time-stop 21d

Crypto account · 24/7

Crypto Bot cryptobot-*

Fully segregated: its own $1M paper account, its own keys, its own schedule — crypto never sleeps, so neither does it. Runs trend-filtered RSI mean reversion (buy <30 in an uptrend, sell >55) on BTC/USD and ETH/USD, sizing each entry at 15% of equity. It trades around a permanent ~$500k core — 70/30 BTC/ETH bought as the crypto twin of the stock account's SPY layer — plus a 26-coin alt sleeve, and it can only ever sell coins from its own tagged book. The core is untouchable.

04

The Risk Engine

Autonomous money needs autonomous brakes. Every buy passes through shared, account-wide gates in risk.py before it reaches the broker — sells are never blocked, because the exit door stays open.

🛑

Drawdown kill switch

All buying halts if equity falls more than 5% below its high-water mark — computed statelessly from the broker's own portfolio history, so it can't be gamed by a bot with amnesia.

ARMED · −2.08% vs HWM
💧

Cash reserve floor

No buy may take cash below 10% of equity. The dip-buyer always keeps dry powder for the next dip instead of going all-in on the first one.

ARMED · 15.5% held
📉

Daily loss halt

Bot v2 stops buying for the day if the account drops 2% intraday. Tomorrow is another day; today is done.

ARMED · day −1.19%
📏

Position & order caps

No single name over 5% of equity, max 5 orders per run, no trading in the first or last 5 minutes of the session — no fat fingers, even robotic ones.

STRUCTURAL
🏷️

Book isolation

Every order is tagged. A bot can only sell shares its own tag bought — it can't touch the SPY layer, the crypto core, another bot's book, or my manual trades.

STRUCTURAL
🧪

Promotion rule

Strategies aren't hot-swapped on one good backtest. A challenger must beat the incumbent in-sample and out-of-sample in two consecutive monthly tournaments — and a human flips the switch.

05

How a trade happens

From cron tick to phone buzz, with no human in between.

01

A Routine wakes up

The cron fires and a fresh AI agent spins up, checks out main — the single source of truth — and installs dependencies. Feature branches never trade.

02

The bot reads the market

Completed hourly bars only — the still-forming bar is dropped so signals can't peek at the future. RSI, z-scores, and trend filters decide: signal or no signal.

03

Risk gates vote

Cash reserve, drawdown kill switch, position caps, market-window checks. A BLOCKED line in the log is the system working, not failing.

04

Order → broker → verify

Tagged market order to the Alpaca paper API. The agent then re-reads the order status from the broker and reports the actual fill — never the intended one.

05

The human gets one line

A push notification: 🪙 BTC/USD: RSI 27.3 OVERSOLD → bought $150,000. The nightly report and the every-2-hours watchdog keep the receipts.

06

What this doesn't prove

Everything above is the system working as designed. This section is the part that doesn't. A backtest picked these strategies, and the backtest has real defects — one of them a bug I found by re-reading my own out-of-sample split. Written down here because a number you haven't tried to break isn't evidence.

BUG · confirmed

My out-of-sample test recomputes indicators on the slice

The tournament splits each symbol 70/30 and scores the tail as out-of-sample. But it calls strategy(part) on the truncated frame, so every rolling window restarts from scratch inside the test set instead of carrying history across the split.

A 200-bar trend filter on a 110-bar out-of-sample window is entirely undefined — the strategy can never signal and scores as flat, which reads as "no edge" when it actually means "never ran." Reproducing it on 1,200 synthetic hourly bars: the first 199 bars of every out-of-sample window are unconditionally flat, and the strategy takes 2 round trips as written against 3 with the indicator computed on full history — 37 bars in the market instead of 68.

The strategy this hits hardest is rsi_reversion_trend — the trend-filtered dip logic Bot v2 actually trades. The out-of-sample number meant to validate the live strategy is the least trustworthy number in the table.

strategies.pyWARMUP_BARS = 200 # declared, and never imported by backtest.py

Fix: compute signals on the full series, then slice — or enforce the warmup constant that's already sitting there unused.

BIAS

I picked the four symbols already knowing how they did

The default universe is TSLA, NVDA, PLTR and SPY — names that are famous in 2026 precisely because they went up. A long-only dip-buyer tested on assets with strong upward drift looks good almost regardless of whether the signal has merit: buy any dip in a rising asset and wait.

Nothing in the sample was delisted, halted, or went to zero, so the tournament has never once been asked what the strategy does when a name simply doesn't come back. Keeping buy_and_hold in the tournament as a control was the right instinct — but the control can't rescue a universe chosen with hindsight.

Fix: a survivorship-free universe including names that died, and a point-in-time index membership list rather than today's winners.

STATISTICS

The winner is the maximum of 44 noisy estimates

Eleven strategies across four symbols, ranked by average Sharpe. The maximum of many noisy estimates is biased upward even when every strategy in the pool is worthless — the more variants I try, the better the best one looks for reasons that have nothing to do with edge.

The promotion rule genuinely helps here, and it's the part of this project I'd defend: a challenger must win in-sample and out-of-sample in two consecutive monthly tournaments before a human flips the switch. But the monthly re-runs pull overlapping windows, so the same "out-of-sample" data gets reused every month and decays into in-sample.

Fix: deflate the Sharpe for the number of trials, and hold back a window the tournament is never allowed to see until a strategy is up for promotion.

STATISTICS

Annualizing a two-month Sharpe

The default run is 59 days of 5-minute bars, annualized by sqrt(78 × 252). That scaling assumes bar returns are independent — but a strategy that holds a position across many bars produces heavily autocorrelated returns, so the annualized figure overstates. Two months is also a single regime. The resulting confidence interval comfortably contains zero, and I was reading these numbers to one decimal place.

Fix: report the Sharpe with an error bar, or don't annualize a sample this short.

COSTS

Costs are a flat haircut, and fills are free

5 bps per side on stocks, 25 on crypto, charged per position flip. No market impact, no queue position, no partial fills, no spread widening. Spreads blow out exactly when a mean-reversion signal fires — the model charges the same 5 bps in a calm tape as in a panic, which is the one moment it matters.

Live is no stricter: paper fills market orders against the quote with no impact and no rejection, and signals come off the free IEX feed, which is a fraction of consolidated volume rather than the NBBO a real order would meet. A $150k crypto order clearing instantly at the touch is not a thing that happens to real money.

Fix: model cost as a function of spread and realized volatility, and haircut paper fills before believing any of it.

MISMATCH

The backtest and the live bot don't trade at the same moment

The backtester assumes execution at the bar close. The live Routine fires at 35 minutes past the hour and acts on the most recently completed bar — so a real entry happens minutes after, and at a different price from, the one the backtest scored. Small on hourly bars. Not zero, and it always cuts the same direction on fast-moving names.

DATA

The backtester silently changes data source

get_bars catches any Alpaca failure and falls back to Yahoo with a warning on stderr. The monthly tournament runs without keys — so it re-ranks strategies on Yahoo data, with different adjustments and session boundaries, and then reports on strategies that trade Alpaca data. The source is recorded in df.attrs and never printed in the ranking, so a mixed-source comparison looks identical to a clean one.

Fix: tag every result with its source and refuse to rank across sources.

Two things that actually broke

RESOLVED

I committed API keys and GitHub revoked them for me

An early commit carried Alpaca credentials. GitHub's secret scanning caught them and auto-revoked the pair before I noticed — the failure mode that saved me is the one I should have built. Keys now live outside the repository entirely and never touch a commit, private repo or not.

RESOLVED

Two bots on one account traded against each other

The RSI bot and Bot v2 shared a stock account, and nothing stopped one from selling what the other had just bought — or from either touching the passive SPY layer. Fixed by partitioning: every order carries its own tag, each bot may only sell what its own tag bought, TSLA/NVDA/PLTR belong exclusively to the RSI bot, and the index layer belongs to no bot at all. Account-wide limits moved out of both bots into a shared risk module, because a limit that lives inside one bot doesn't bind the other.

So what do the numbers say?

Three days in, the stock account is behind SPY by $1,070 and behind its own frozen twin by $966 — that second gap is the honest one, because it says the trading itself has subtracted value rather than the picks being wrong. Crypto is up 0.70%, almost entirely from a core position the bot isn't allowed to sell.

None of that means anything yet. Three days is noise. A win would have been noise too, and I'd like to think I'd be saying so with the same confidence. The reason to publish it is that the measurement runs nightly, automatically, against benchmarks chosen before the result was known — so in six months there'll be something here worth reading, whichever way it goes.
